Ethan Meyer - Pre-Course Reflection - 8/24/2020

As I enter my second year of school and begin my journey with PLC, I am overwhelmed with conflicting dualities in my thoughts and emotions. I am both excited and nervous. I am full of energy, yet exhausted at the same time. Beginning this entry, I feel it necessary to disclose such thoughts at this time. I believe this class offers me the opportunity to add skills to my toolbelt of leadership. In the book “Good to Great”, author Jim Collins posits the concept of level five leadership. This leadership style is neither dominant nor submissive; it is a hybrid that fosters reverence. My leadership style, as it is now, is one that tends to sway towards the dominant side. I know that in order to be a more effective leader, I must continually add new tools with which I can approach novel situations. I believe both curriculum and experience fostered through this class will help me refine my skills and ultimately lead me down the path towards becoming a level five leader. I bring with myself a dedication to justice that I believe will benefit PRLC 1810 and the greater PLC community. I believe this value helps me listen to the opinions of others and process it thoroughly rather than forming snap judgements.. That being said, I will be sure to maintain a respectful discourse with students at all times. I believe these values are important because it is only through justice that oppressed voices can be heard, that meaningful discussions can be had, and ultimately, that progress can be achieved.

Ethan Meyer - Post-Course Reflection - 12/6/2020

Through the combination of constructive conversations and meaningful coursework, I have both developed and supplemented my character and leadership potential. Although there were countless incidents of personal growth over the last five months, I will focus on a few that had the greatest impact.
PRLC 1810 has provided an arena in which I felt comfortable discussing controversial and touchy issues. From racial descrimation, to gender inequality, there was never a shortage of intense moments. By having such deep discussions with my peers, this class gives me hope for my greater communities. Even in a nation where hate and polarization are on the rise, when discussions, and especially the people in them, are respectful, a great deal of progress can be made.
Throughout the semester, I relearned the value of patience – especially with myself. In so many ways, this class is challenging. It challenged everything from my thoughts and beliefs, to the core of my character. Effort in a class like this comes in many shapes and sizes including but not limited to engagement, vulnerability, and sacrifice. But these ideals are a lot easier said than done. For example, it took me time to be willing to expose feelings I wasn’t comfortable sharing with just anyone. Furthermore, I learned to be patient with myself when I struggle. I am a pure bred over achiever. The “Who Am I?” paper reminded me of this. Rewarded with good grades, varsity letters, trophies, and eventually a life-changing scholarship, society has reinforced these habits. It is for this reason that my biggest fear lies within underperformance. This class has pushed me into developing a more nuanced framework for approaching failure that I know will prove to be valuable in my future.
Second, I have realized that the morals behind my beliefs must be the only motivator behind my actions. At times, this class can be depressing. I remember there was a point around halfway through the semester where I struggled to engage in our discussions. After talking about some big, hairy ethical problems, I left classes feeling as if there was nothing I could do to initiate change. The conversations felt meaningless because I believed any action I took would be futile in trying to resist. My growth occured when I realized that the scope of my influence should have no impact on the choices I make. In other words, I should choose to do the right thing not because I know it will have an enormous impact, but because it is the right thing to do. I have come to grips with the fact that I am not the godsend of humanity. I have also found great comfort in knowing that my good deeds are enough to satisfy my desire to change the world. As the semester draws to a close, I know that although my time in the classroom is ending, the pursuit of leadership is neverending. Going forward, I must continue to grow through initiating constructive conversations and seeking other challenges.
